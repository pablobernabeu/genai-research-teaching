# Facilitator guide — *Generative AI in research and teaching*

A practical, critical featured workshop in two parts (30 + 30 minutes, split by a
networking lunch) for university researchers and educators.

> **Read me first.** This is the facilitator's operating manual: the framing, the
> objectives, the minute-by-minute schedule, the prompts to circulate, the
> mid-point pivot and the contingencies. Pair it with the lighter-weight
> [run sheet](docs/facilitator_run_sheet.md) and [morning checklist](docs/morning_checklist.md)
> for the day itself.

---

## 1. The stance

The guiding posture is **critical, evidence-oriented and balanced**. The aim is
**discernment, not enthusiasm**. Participants are accomplished researchers; treat
them as expert peers who happen to be new to a tool, not as novices to be sold a
product.

The aim is **deeper insight into the technology**, not technical instruction on any
one tool — and everyone is welcome, from those who have never opened a chatbot to
daily users. The kit also places AI in context: generative AI is the latest in a
long line of tools — mental arithmetic to the calculator, pen and paper to the
computer, deterministic software to generative AI — that a productivity-driven
economy keeps pushing us towards. For better or worse, our institutions reward
output; the question is not whether to reach for such tools but how to do so with
discernment.

A single thread runs through the session: **the friction a researcher feels when a
tool resists their intent is a useful epistemic signal, not a nuisance**. When an
assistant gives a glib answer, flattens a hard distinction or invents a citation,
that resistance marks exactly where the researcher's judgement is doing real work.
The whole design asks participants to **record friction rather than smooth it
away**.

This is not a contrarian position. There is evidence on both sides, and the kit
keeps both in view:

- Generative AI demonstrably reduces effort on knowledge tasks — and, in the same
  study, **higher confidence in the tool went with measurably less critical
  thinking**, while higher confidence in one's own expertise went with more
  (Lee et al., 2025).
- Reintroducing friction on purpose — brief "**provocations**" that question an
  output — **restores** critical and metacognitive engagement (Drosos et al., 2025).

So the design is itself an intervention: we build friction back in, and we observe
what it surfaces.

## 2. Learning objectives

By the end of the session a participant should be able to:

1. **Locate** a given task on the tool spectrum — off-the-shelf, no-code or
   IDE/API level — and **justify** the choice against control, data exposure and
   effort.
2. **Recognise** cognitive friction as an epistemic signal and **record** it,
   rather than treating it as an obstacle.
3. **Apply** the data-security red lines and a simple decision aid *before*
   submitting anything to a third-party tool.
4. **Identify** the principal ethical tensions and **name** the disclosure and
   accountability implications for their own work.
5. **Design** an explicit human-in-the-loop protocol for a real task — mapping the
   automated phases against the human-steered ones, and deciding whether oversight
   is interwoven through the project or staged at distinct checkpoints.

The five objectives map one-to-one onto the five group roles and the five rubric
dimensions, so every objective has an owner in each group and a place in the
written record.

## 3. Pedagogical design

- **Two parts, with a real break.** A 30-minute conceptual first part builds the
  lens and ends by forming groups; a 45-minute networking lunch; a 30-minute
  applied second part puts the lens to work.
- **Problem-based.** Groups bring a real, recurring problem (anonymised or
  synthesised) from their research or teaching. Authentic tasks expose authentic
  friction; toy tasks do not.
- **Collaborative.** Groups of **five** — large enough that every group's insight
  fits the lightning round, small enough that everyone works on the problem. Rotating
  attention, not fixed ownership; peers catch each other's over-trust.
- **Critical by construction.** Part 2 splits **building** from **interrogating** at
  a brief pivot mid-way: the first stretch earns the experience, the second puts it
  on trial.
- **Senior-appropriate.** Short framing, quick to apply, room to run. The
  facilitator circulates with questions, not answers.
- **Inclusive by design.** Part 1 gives newcomers the practical grounding (what these
  tools are, how to get one, how to ask) and everyone the critical lens, then previews
  the rubric — so a mixed-ability group dives into Part 2 together and no one meets
  the task cold.
- **Reproducible and open.** Every group's reasoning is written into a shared note
  and archived publicly, so the cohort's collective judgement outlasts the session.

## 5. Phase-by-phase notes

### 5.1 Part 1 · Conceptualising the use of AI in research · 12:00–12:30 · slides 3–17

A 30-minute conceptual session that **sets up the groupwork**. You are building a
shared lens and giving everyone — newcomer to power user — the confidence to take
part in Part 2. Keep it brisk and a little interactive.

- **Welcome and disclaimer** (slide 2) — read the disclaimer aloud; it sets the tone
  that accountability stays with us.
- **The stance** (slide 5) — discernment, not enthusiasm; the aim is deeper insight,
  not tool instruction; whatever their confidence, everyone is welcome.
- **"Saved or burned"** (slide 6) — a sentence each with a neighbour; primes the
  room and surfaces real, recent friction. Keep it to two minutes.
- **A long line of tools** (slide 7) — place AI in context: the latest in a history
  of productivity tools (calculator, computer, spreadsheet, generative AI) in an
  economy that rewards output. The frame for the day: not whether to use it, but how
  to use it with discernment.
- **Getting started** (slide 8) — the inclusive floor: what a chat assistant is, pick
  one free tool, the shape of a useful request, and that mixed-ability groups are a
  strength. Newcomers need this to get the most from Part 2; say it plainly, then move on.
- **Friction as signal** (slide 9) — the spine. Cite Lee et al. and Drosos et al. in
  a sentence each; note it bears on marking and writing as much as analysis.
- **The spectrum, red lines and ethics** (slides 10–12) — reference frames for Part 2,
  with the teaching angle named (students' data, assessment integrity, disclosure).
- **The bigger questions** (slide 13) — the societal lens: the thinking/writing
  shift, fairness (who is helped, who is left out — language, support, career stage,
  background) and disclosure. Pose them as live questions to carry into the
  groupwork, not to settle now.
- **Human-in-the-loop and the map** (slide 14) — accountability stays human; preview
  interwoven vs staged.
- **What Part 2 asks, then form groups** (slides 15–16) — show that the concepts just
  covered *are* the rubric and the societal reflection they will fill (slide 15),
  then have people **form groups of five now** by gathering at a seed idea (slide 16).
  This is the one piece of set-up in Part 1; each group settles on its **one** problem —
  that seed, or a real one a member brought — in the first minute of Part 2.

Resist over-running; the conceptual content is the floor for the applied session, not
a lecture to complete. Every concept should land as something they will *use* in
Part 2, not just hear.

### 5.2 The lunch · 12:30–13:15

A networking break. Groups were formed at the end of Part 1, so Part 2 can begin
straight away.

### 5.3 Part 2 · Practical AI for research and teaching · 13:15–13:45 · slides 18–28

Your job is to **circulate**, not present. Keep the "Go" slide (26) or the Part 2
timeline (slide 19) displayed and use the prompt bank in §6.

- **13:15–13:18 Settle in.** Groups re-find each other after lunch, open a HackMD
  note with the compact template, and **agree their one problem in about 60 seconds —
  the seed they formed around, or a real one a member brought**. Re-state the red
  lines.
- **13:18–13:33 Apply it.** Let them work. Intervene only to (a) stop sensitive data
  going into a tool, (b) unstick a group that cannot start, or (c) drop a single
  provocation. Around **13:26**, run the brief pivot (§5.4). Note good caught errors
  for the synthesis, and make sure each group sketches its **automation–steering
  map** and decides whether oversight is interwoven or staged.
- **13:33–13:40 Lightning round.** See §5.5.
- **13:40–13:45 Close.** See §5.6.

This is a compressed applied session: depth over coverage. One genuine caught error and one
clear human checkpoint beat a half-finished artefact.

### 5.4 The pivot · around 13:26 (slide 27)

Mid-way through the applied session, call a 30-second halt — keyboards down. Say, roughly:

> "Thirty seconds, keyboards down. What has the tool made **genuinely** easier, and
> what does that cost? Where did it **resist** you — and what was that friction
> telling you? Have you held the **data red lines**? Now **switch mode**: from
> building to interrogating. Lock in your map and your one insight."

It is the hinge: groups that have been enjoying the tool now put it on trial.

### 5.5 Lightning round · 13:33–13:40 · slide 28

A **facilitator-timed** round, not full presentations.

- **One 45-second spoken insight per group. No slides.** The Reporter picks the sharpest
  of three: *the most significant limitation you found; the most important
  human-in-the-loop safeguard you built in; or one honest thing this exposed about how your
  field is over- or under-using AI for this task.* The third option is where the
  field-level reflection gets voiced from the work just done — offer it, never force it.
- Keep a visible timer. At 45 seconds, thank them and move on.
- **Timing maths:** about seven minutes — enough for **8–10 groups** at 45 seconds
  each to all give an insight. Hold the timer firmly; thank a group at 45 seconds and
  move on so every group fits. Every group's written note also enters the archive.
- As each group speaks, jot the recurring thread (over-trust, hallucinated
  citations, data leakage, de-skilling, a clever checkpoint) for the synthesis.

### 5.6 Close · 13:40–13:45 · slides 29–35

Synthesise, do not summarise. Pull two or three concrete insights from the lightning
round into the through-line (slide 31). Land the threads: friction as signal, the
tool spectrum, the data red lines, the human-in-the-loop and the bigger questions.
Give the **take-home actions** (slide 32), point to the **further reading** (slide 33)
and the **follow-up email**, and close on time at 13:45.

## 6. Circulating prompt bank

Carry these. They are tuned to the sub-phases and to the five rubric dimensions.
Ask one, listen, move on.

**Project Definition**
- "What is the actual problem here — in one sentence a colleague would recognise?"
- "How will you know the tool helped? What is your success criterion?"

**Technology Stack**
- "Why this tool and not one a level up or down the spectrum?"
- "What would you lose, and gain, by moving to a no-code or API approach?"

**Data Security & Ethics**
- "What exactly are you about to paste in? Whose data is it?"
- "If this note were public tomorrow — and it will be — is anything in the tool you would regret?"
- "Where would you need to disclose that you used AI for this?"

**Financial & Scalability Constraints**
- "Does this still work on the free tier next month, for fifty people?"
- "What breaks first when you scale this — cost, limits or trust?"

**Human-in-the-Loop Protocol**
- "Where, precisely, does a human have to check before this is usable?"
- "What will you never let the tool decide?"
- "Map it: which phases are automated, and where do you steer? Is your oversight interwoven through every step or staged at checkpoints — and why that choice?"

**Societal reflection (the bigger picture)**
- "If the tool wrote it, where did your thinking go — and what would you lose by leaning on it?"
- "Does this widen or narrow a gap — language, support, career stage, background? Who gains, who is left behind?"
- "Would you disclose using AI here? Would you disclose what it compensated for?"

**Friction (the spine — ask everywhere)**
- "Where did it resist you? Don't fix it yet — what is that resistance telling you?"
- "What did it get confidently wrong? Is that in your museum yet?"

## 7. Contingencies

| If… | Then… |
|---|---|
| **Wi-Fi fails** | Switch the affected groups to the offline path: critique a printed worked example (`docs/worked_examples.md`) against the rubric. The thinking, not the tool, is the deliverable. |
| **A chosen tool demands payment or a login wall** | Move down the spectrum to a free off-the-shelf chat tool; treat the paywall itself as a finding under Financial & Scalability. |
| **HackMD is blocked or refuses sign-up** | Groups draft in a shared document or on paper; the facilitator transcribes one note into the archive afterwards. The rubric template is plain Markdown and portable. |
| **A group finishes early** | Send them to the next rubric dimension they scored lowest on, or set them to red-team a neighbouring group's output — to probe it adversarially for weaknesses the other group missed. |
| **A group stalls** | Shrink the task. "Forget the grand version — what is the smallest real thing the tool could help with in ten minutes?" |
| **Uneven groups (4 or 6)** | At four, the Convenor doubles as Steward. At six, split Driver and Sceptic across two people, or add a second Sceptic — more red-teaming is no bad thing. |
| **Fewer or more than 10 groups** | The lightning round is about seven minutes. Take 45 seconds each for as many groups as fit, then a one-line harvest from the rest; never overrun the 13:45 finish. |
| **Someone pastes sensitive data** | Stop it gently and immediately, note it as a live example of why the red lines exist and have them anonymise or synthesise before continuing. |
| **A dominant voice** | Lean on roles: "Steward, what is your read?" Give quieter roles explicit air. |
| **A sceptical or hostile participant** | Welcome it — scepticism is the point. Channel it into the Sceptic role and the museum. |
| **AV or logo issues** | The deck is self-contained; the placeholder logo is embedded. If projection fails, the instructions are all in `project_tracks.md` and the rubric template. |
