# Pre-workshop poll (short, anonymous)

A 60-second warm-up to gauge the room and pitch the session well. Keep it
**anonymous** and **optional**, and collect **no personal data** — no names, no
email addresses, no free-text that could identify anyone.

## Tooling

Use a free, anonymous form tool the institution permits. **Microsoft Forms** is the
simplest here — it comes with an Oxford (Nexus365) account — with **Google Forms** an
equally good fallback. The setting that matters: make the form **open to anyone** (in
Microsoft Forms, *Settings → Who can fill out this form → Anyone can respond*) — this
both lets external attendees in and keeps responses anonymous; check that no name or
email is recorded. Avoid Mentimeter here: its free tier caps at **50 participants a
month** (a 40–50-person cohort would exhaust it) and it is built for live in-room
polling, not an asynchronous pre-read. Put the link in the
[pre-workshop email](pre_workshop_email.md) as **[POLL URL]**.

## The questions

1. **How often do you currently use generative AI tools in your work?**
   Never · Tried once or twice · Occasionally · Weekly · Daily

2. **Where would you put yourself today?**
   Curious sceptic · Cautious user · Comfortable user · Power user · Unsure

3. **Which best describes what you most want from the session?**
   Practical skills · A critical framework · Data and ethics confidence ·
   The bigger questions (fairness, disclosure, what AI does to thinking) ·
   Time-saving ideas · Just to see what is possible

4. **How comfortable are you that you know the data red lines** — what should
   never go into a free AI tool?
   Not at all · Somewhat · Fairly · Very

5. **Which area would you most like to work on at the workshop?**
   Methods and analysis · Admin and planning · Building or prototyping ·
   Public communication · Not sure yet

6. *(Optional, anonymous)* **In a few words, one hope and one worry about AI in your
   research.**
   *Tell respondents not to include anything identifying.*

## Using the results

- A show of Q1/Q2 sets the level — mostly sceptics, mostly users or a wide mix.
- Q3 and Q5 tell you which tracks and framings to emphasise — and how many choose **the bigger questions** signals how hard to lean on the societal thread.
- Q4 calibrates how much time to give the data red lines.
- A couple of anonymous Q6 lines make an honest opening slide or a line in the
  introduction — "someone hopes…, someone worries…".

Share a one-line summary of the results in the introduction; it signals you
listened, and it models transparency about how data is used.
