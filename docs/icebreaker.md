# Icebreaker — senior-appropriate, for Part 1

A short, dignified opener for a room of experienced academics. It runs **early in
Part 1 (around 12:03), with a neighbour**, so it **adds no time** to the schedule —
it is slide 6. Groups of five do not exist yet (they form at the end of Part 1), so this is a
quick pair or trio exchange, not a table round. It seeds the day's thesis — that the
friction in our AI encounters is worth attending to — without anyone having to
perform.

## Primary · "Saved or burned" (≈ 2 minutes)

On screen as Part 1 gets going:

> **In one sentence each, with the person next to you:** the last time an AI tool
> either *saved you* or *burned you* — and which it was.

- One sentence per person, no debate — just trade stories.
- It surfaces real, recent experience and sets a candid tone.
- Two minutes, in parallel, then pull the room back. A couple of volunteered
  lines make a good bridge into "friction is a signal".

**Why it fits the cohort.** It assumes expertise and recent first-hand use, invites
a wry war story rather than a confession, and needs no preparation or AI knowledge —
"I have not really used one" is itself a valid, interesting answer.

## Alternate · "One hope, one suspicion" (≈ 3 minutes)

If the room is quieter or more sceptical:

> **With your neighbour, a sentence each:** one *hope* and one *suspicion* you hold
> about AI in your research or teaching.

- Names the ambivalence in the room out loud and legitimises scepticism early —
  useful, because scepticism is exactly what the session wants to channel.

## Facilitator note

Do **not** run this as a whole-room go-around — with 40–50 people that would eat
into the 30 minutes. Keep it strictly neighbour-level and parallel. If the room is
slow to settle, let it lapse and move on; momentum matters more than completeness.
