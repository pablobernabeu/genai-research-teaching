# The four project tracks and the rubric — at a glance

A friendly overview of the core of Part 2, the applied session, for sharing with
organisers and colleagues. The full working briefs are in
[`project_tracks.md`](../project_tracks.md) and
[`evaluation_rubric_template.md`](../evaluation_rubric_template.md).

In Part 2, each group of five takes **one real problem** (or a ready example) and works
it through one of four tracks, then puts the result on trial against a five-dimension
rubric and a short societal reflection. Every track works for a research *or* a teaching
task, and everything runs on **free browser tools** — nothing to install, no prior
experience assumed.

## The four tracks

Each track is a different honest test of where generative AI helps a researcher, and
where it must not be trusted. A group picks one.

- **A · Methodological Blind-Spot Detector.** Turn the tool into a sceptical reviewer of
  your own design, then do the harder step: verify which of its critiques are *real*,
  which are generic and which are simply wrong. *For example: stress-test a methods
  section or an assessment.*
- **B · Accessible Executive-Function Layer.** Build a reusable AI routine for the
  planning, triage and summarising that fills a research week, and decide where the
  scaffolding should stop before it quietly de-skills. *For example: turn messy notes
  into owned actions, or tame an inbox backlog.*
- **C · Rapid Prototyping for Knowledge Translation.** Build a small working artefact —
  an explainer, a chart, a simple interactive — from a prompt in minutes, then verify it
  as publication would demand (the "looks right, subtly wrong" trap). *For example: a
  one-page explainer or a quick visualisation of a finding.*
- **D · Public Engagement Translator.** Produce audience-tailored versions of a public
  finding, then audit them for fidelity — over-claims, lost caveats, false certainty.
  *For example: a lay summary, a social thread or a schools explainer.*

## The rubric — five dimensions

Groups self-assess against five dimensions, each rated **1–5** (**1 Nascent · 3
Developing · 5 Robust**). It is a reflection scaffold, not a competition — a thoughtful
3 with a sharp caught error beats an unexamined 5.

1. **Project Definition** — is the real problem, and its success criterion, clear?
2. **Technology Stack** — the right tool on the spectrum, and why?
3. **Data Security & Ethics** — red lines held; UK GDPR, ethics and fairness addressed?
4. **Financial & Scalability** — does the free tier hold; what breaks at scale?
5. **Human-in-the-Loop** — explicit checkpoints, verification and accountability, with a
   deliberate map of what is automated against where the human steers?

## A short societal reflection (not scored)

Alongside the rating, each group reflects on the bigger picture:

- **Thinking vs writing** — if the tool did the writing, what thinking did we still do,
  and what might we lose by offloading it?
- **Fairness** — could this widen or narrow disparities (first vs second language, more
  vs less support, career stage, background)? Who gains, and who is left behind?
- **Disclosure** — would we disclose using AI here, and would we disclose the conditions
  it offset?

## And one spoken insight

Each group closes with a **45-second** spoken insight, no slides: the single most
significant limitation they found, or the most important human-in-the-loop safeguard
they built in.
