# Pre-workshop briefing email

> Send 5–7 days ahead. Plain text or light HTML. Replace every bracketed placeholder. Keep it short — the aim is to lower nerves and set expectations, not to pre-teach. Paragraphs are single continuous lines so the body pastes cleanly into an email client.

Subject: Before our AI workshop on 24 June 2026 — a short read

Dear colleague,

Thank you for signing up to Generative AI in research and teaching on 24 June 2026 at [VENUE]. It runs in two parts: Part 1, Conceptualising the use of AI in research (12:00–12:30); a networking lunch (12:30–13:15); then Part 2, Practical AI for research and teaching (13:15–13:45), an applied session. There is nothing to install, and whatever your confidence with these tools — from never having tried one to daily use — you are welcome; no prior experience with AI tools is assumed.

Generative AI is the newest in a long line of tools we have always used to extend what we can do — from mental arithmetic to the calculator, pen and paper to the computer, deterministic software to generative AI. This session offers a deeper insight into the technology itself, rather than training in any one tool. We will do two things at once: put these tools to work and learn to read them well — because the real skill is knowing where they genuinely help and where your judgement leads. Your expertise is what makes them valuable: fluent and confident as they are, they are not always right, and you are well placed to tell the difference. And we will open up the wider questions they raise — how our thinking shifts when the tool shares the writing, how AI might narrow or widen disparities (a second language, uneven support, career stage, background) and what, in the end, we choose to disclose.

You do not need to prepare anything — in Part 2 your group can work from a ready example we provide for each track. The session is more rewarding on a real problem, though, so if you have a recurring one from your research or teaching, come with it in mind: a methods section or assessment to stress-test, an admin burden to lighten, a finding or concept to make accessible, a dataset to visualise. Your group works just one problem, so treat anything you bring as a candidate it might take up rather than a commitment. Whatever you bring must be non-confidential, or something you can anonymise first — so please do not bring personal data, students' marks or references, unpublished participant data, confidential grant or peer-review material, or anything under embargo.

In Part 2 you will work in groups of five and write up your group's note in a simple browser app (genai-rt.web.app). There is nothing to install and no account to create: your group names itself, enters a passcode I read out on the day, fills in its note and submits it for me to review. If a group would rather not use the app, there is a fallback in HackMD, a free browser-based notepad, and a paper fallback too. Afterwards I tidy the approved notes into an open, public archive of the day.

What to bring:

- A laptop and charger (the room has power and Wi-Fi throughout).
- Optionally, a real problem to work on (non-confidential; see above).
- Optionally, a free account with one chat assistant (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini or Copilot) — it saves a few minutes, but there is time on the day. You do not need an account for the note-taking app.
- Curiosity and scepticism. Both are welcome.

Optional, if time allows: two short pieces that anticipate the day's themes. A reading — Kristi Girdharry, "A writing professor's new task in the age of AI: Teaching students when to struggle" (The Conversation, 2026): https://theconversation.com/a-writing-professors-new-task-in-the-age-of-ai-teaching-students-when-to-struggle-276590 — on why the friction of thinking unaided is where discernment begins. And a talk — Advait Sarkar, a Cambridge and Microsoft Research researcher on AI and thinking: "How to Stop AI from Killing Your Critical Thinking" (TED, 2025): https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3lPnN8omdPA.

And on the shift itself — writing and programming moving up a level, with you leading the ideation and steering at the key moments: Andrej Karpathy, "Software is changing (again)" (a talk, 2025): https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LCEmiRjPEtQ; and Ethan Mollick, "Centaurs and cyborgs on the jagged frontier" (2023): https://www.oneusefulthing.org/p/centaurs-and-cyborgs-on-the-jagged.

No view here is the last word — the technology moves too fast for that. Please bring your own critical, informed perspective to our shared tables.
